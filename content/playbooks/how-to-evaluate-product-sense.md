## Executive Summary

"Product sense" gets used as a catch-all for "I liked how they thought," which makes it nearly impossible to evaluate consistently. This playbook breaks product sense into its component parts — problem framing, trade-off reasoning, user empathy, and outcome orientation — so it can be evaluated the same way for every candidate, not vibes-checked.

## Why This Matters

When product sense is evaluated as a single gut feeling, it becomes a proxy for "reminds me of myself," which quietly biases hiring toward people who think and communicate like the interviewer. Breaking it into components makes the evaluation more accurate and more fair, and gives candidates a clearer target to prepare against.

## The Refine Framework

Product sense is the composite of four distinct skills. Evaluate each separately.

1. **Problem framing** — Can the candidate correctly identify the actual problem, as opposed to the first plausible-sounding one? Strong candidates slow down and ask what problem is really being solved before proposing solutions.
2. **Trade-off reasoning** — Can the candidate name what they're giving up with a given choice, not just what they're gaining? Product sense shows up in the quality of the trade-offs considered, not the cleverness of the final answer.
3. **User empathy** — Does the candidate reason from a specific user's actual situation, or from an abstract, undifferentiated "the user"? Specificity here is the signal.
4. **Outcome orientation** — Does the candidate tie their proposal back to a measurable business or user outcome, or does the reasoning stop at "this would be a better experience"?

A candidate can be strong in some of these and weak in others. Naming which is which turns a vague "good product sense" into an actionable evaluation.

## How Hiring Managers Think

The instinct in a product sense interview is to evaluate the final answer. Resist it. The final answer is the least informative part of the conversation — there are usually several reasonable answers to any open-ended product question. What differentiates candidates is the reasoning that gets them there: what they asked about first, what they ruled out and why, and whether they noticed the actual constraint in the prompt.

The single highest-value move in this kind of interview is watching whether the candidate asks clarifying questions before proposing a solution. Candidates who rush to an answer are optimizing for looking decisive; candidates who ask two or three sharp clarifying questions first are usually demonstrating the instinct you actually want on the job.

## How Candidates Should Think

Don't try to arrive at the "correct" answer — in most product sense interviews, there isn't one. Try to make your reasoning visible. State the problem you think you're solving before proposing anything, and explicitly flag when you're making an assumption to keep moving. When you propose a solution, name at least one thing you're trading away, and one way you'd measure whether it worked.

Slow down more than feels natural. The instinct under interview pressure is to fill silence with an answer. Interviewers evaluating product sense are almost always more impressed by a thoughtful pause and a clarifying question than by a fast, confident, under-considered answer.

## Common Mistakes

- **Interviewers scoring the answer instead of the reasoning.** Two candidates can reach the same answer with very different quality of thinking behind it.
- **Candidates skipping clarifying questions to seem decisive.** This usually backfires — it reads as a lack of rigor, not confidence.
- **Interviewers rewarding familiarity of framework over quality of thought.** A candidate reciting a named framework isn't necessarily reasoning better than one who isn't.
- **Candidates reasoning about an abstract user instead of a specific one.** "Users want simplicity" is weaker than "a first-time user on mobile, mid-commute, wants to complete this in under thirty seconds."

## Real Examples

**Weak:** Asked to improve a checkout flow, the candidate immediately proposes three features without asking who the user is, what the current drop-off point is, or what's already been tried.

**Strong:** The candidate asks where in the funnel users are dropping off, whether this is mobile or desktop, and what's already been tested, before proposing anything. When they do propose a change, they explicitly note the trade-off ("this adds a step, which could hurt completion for the segment that's already converting well") and suggest how they'd measure it.

## Interview Questions

- "How would you improve [product]?"
- "What's a product decision you'd have made differently than the team that made it?"
- "Walk me through how you'd prioritize between these three initiatives."
- "What's a product you think is well designed, and why?"

## Follow-up Questions

- "What are you assuming here that you're not sure is true?"
- "What would make you wrong about this?"
- "What's the version of this that a different type of user would want instead?"
- "How would you know, two months later, whether this worked?"

## Strong Signals

- Clarifying questions asked before any solution is proposed.
- Explicit trade-off naming without being prompted.
- Reasoning that references a specific user situation, not an abstract one.
- A stated way to measure success before being asked how they'd measure it.

## Weak Signals

- Immediate solutioning with no clarifying questions.
- Vague, universally-applicable answers that could apply to almost any product.
- No mention of trade-offs unless directly asked.

## Red Flags

- Defensiveness when a proposed solution is challenged.
- Inability to name a single downside of their own proposal.
- Reasoning that never touches a measurable outcome.

## Green Flags

- Comfort saying "I'd want more information before committing to an answer," followed by naming exactly what information.
- Willingness to change their answer when given new information mid-conversation.
- Noticing and naming the actual constraint in the prompt, not just the obvious one.

## Decision Framework

Score problem framing, trade-off reasoning, user empathy, and outcome orientation as four separate axes, not one composite "product sense" score. A candidate strong on three of four with a clear gap is easier to calibrate — and easier to coach, if hired — than a single blended impression.

## Preparation Checklist

- [ ] Practice asking 2–3 clarifying questions before proposing any solution.
- [ ] Prepare to name at least one trade-off for any proposal you make.
- [ ] Practice reasoning about a specific user persona, not an abstract "user."
- [ ] Prepare a way to measure success for any solution you propose.

## Hiring Manager Checklist

- [ ] Am I scoring the reasoning process, not just the final answer?
- [ ] Did I note whether clarifying questions were asked before a solution was proposed?
- [ ] Did I probe for trade-offs if the candidate didn't name any?
- [ ] Am I scoring the four components separately rather than one blended impression?

## Candidate Checklist

- [ ] Did I ask clarifying questions before proposing a solution?
- [ ] Did I name at least one trade-off in my proposal?
- [ ] Did I reason about a specific user, not an abstract one?
- [ ] Did I suggest how I'd measure whether my idea worked?

## Key Takeaways

- Product sense is four separable skills: problem framing, trade-off reasoning, user empathy, outcome orientation.
- Evaluate the reasoning process, not the final answer — there's rarely one correct answer.
- Clarifying questions before solutioning are one of the strongest available signals.
- Candidates should slow down, not speed up, when asked an open-ended product question.
