## Executive Summary

Most resume screening is keyword matching disguised as judgment. A better read treats the resume as circumstantial evidence — clues about scope, ownership, and trajectory — that you use to decide what to ask, not what to conclude. This playbook is a framework for reading resumes the way an investigator reads a case file, not the way a search engine reads a query.

## Why This Matters

Keyword matching optimizes for candidates who are good at writing resumes, which correlates only loosely with being good at the job. It also systematically screens out strong candidates whose experience doesn't map neatly onto the job description's language — the person who's done exactly the work you need, described in slightly different words. A better resume read finds signal the keyword scan misses, and treats every resume as a set of hypotheses to test in the interview, not a final verdict.

## The Refine Framework

Read a resume in three passes, each asking a different question.

**Pass one — trajectory.** Look at the sequence of roles and titles over time, not any single one. Is scope increasing? Is the person moving toward more ownership or plateauing? A flat trajectory with strong titles is a different signal than a steep trajectory with modest titles.

**Pass two — verb audit.** Every bullet point uses a verb. "Led," "built," "managed," "contributed to," "supported," "partnered with," "drove" — these verbs are self-reported claims about the ownership band described in [Ownership vs. Participation](/playbooks/ownership-vs-participation). Don't take them at face value, but do take note of the pattern. A resume full of "supported" and "contributed to" is describing a different career than one full of "owned" and "led," even if both people did comparable work.

**Pass three — specificity audit.** Which bullets have numbers, and which don't? Numbers aren't proof of impact, but their absence across an entire resume is a signal worth probing — either the person didn't measure their work, or they're not used to being asked to.

## How Hiring Managers Think

The purpose of the resume read isn't to make a decision. It's to generate a short list of specific things to verify in the interview. If a resume says "led a cross-functional launch," the resume read should produce a follow-up question ("who was on that cross-functional team, and what was your specific role versus theirs"), not a checkbox.

Treat every strong claim on the resume as a hypothesis, and use the interview to test it rather than confirm it. This keeps you from being seduced by good resume-writing and protects you from unfairly discounting a great candidate who's simply not a great writer.

## How Candidates Should Think

Assume your resume will be read this way — as a set of hypotheses, not a final answer — and write it accordingly. Use verbs that accurately reflect your ownership band; don't write "led" for something you participated in, because the gap will surface in the interview and cost you more credibility than an accurate "contributed to" would have. Put a number on as many bullets as you honestly can. If a bullet has no number, be ready to supply one, or an honest reason one doesn't exist, the moment it comes up.

## Common Mistakes

- **Screening out resumes that don't use the job description's exact language.** Strong candidates often describe the same work differently.
- **Treating resume verbs as proof rather than claims to test.** "Led" on a resume and "led" in a verified interview answer are not the same thing.
- **Over-indexing on brand names over described scope.** A recognizable company name is not evidence of individual ownership.
- **Screening for tenure length without asking why moves happened.** Short tenures aren't automatically a red flag; the reason for the move is the actual signal.

## Real Examples

**Resume line:** "Led go-to-market for a new product line, driving $2M in new revenue in the first year."

**What this actually tells you:** the candidate is claiming full ownership and has attached a specific number. It does not yet tell you what "led" meant in practice, whether the number is verifiable, or what part of the go-to-market motion was actually theirs versus a larger team's. The resume read converts this into an interview question: "Walk me through what you personally decided in that go-to-market plan."

## Interview Questions

(To verify resume claims, not to open the interview cold)

- "Your resume says you led X — walk me through what 'led' meant day to day."
- "This bullet doesn't have a number — do you have a rough sense of the impact?"
- "You moved roles after about a year here — what drove that?"

## Follow-up Questions

- "Who else was involved, and what was their role versus yours?"
- "How would you describe this differently if you were writing it today?"

## Strong Signals

- Resume language matches interview answers closely, with consistent ownership claims.
- Numbers on the resume hold up and can be explained in more depth on request.
- Trajectory shows increasing scope, even across different companies or functions.

## Weak Signals

- Significant gap between resume verbs ("led") and what the candidate can actually describe in the interview.
- Numbers that can't be explained or that shift when asked to elaborate.
- A resume with strong titles but consistently vague bullet points.

## Red Flags

- Claims that collapse entirely under a single specific question.
- A pattern of inflated verbs across multiple bullets, not just one.

## Green Flags

- A candidate who proactively clarifies or downgrades a resume claim without being pushed ("that bullet's a bit generous — here's what I actually did").
- Numbers that are specific enough to sound recalled rather than invented for the resume.

## Decision Framework

Use the resume to build your interview question list, not your interview scorecard. A resume should never advance or reject a candidate on its own; it should determine what you ask them.

## Preparation Checklist (Hiring Manager)

- [ ] Read the resume in three passes: trajectory, verb audit, specificity audit.
- [ ] Turn every strong claim into a specific interview question.
- [ ] Note where the resume has no numbers and flag it for a direct question.

## Hiring Manager Checklist

- [ ] Have I generated at least three verification questions from this resume?
- [ ] Am I screening based on described scope, or based on keyword overlap with the job description?
- [ ] Have I considered that a modest resume might describe strong work, poorly written?

## Candidate Checklist

- [ ] Do my resume verbs match my actual ownership band for each bullet?
- [ ] Have I attached a number to every claim I can honestly measure?
- [ ] Can I explain every bullet on my resume in more depth if asked?

## Key Takeaways

- Read resumes for hypotheses to test, not conclusions to accept.
- Trajectory, verb choice, and specificity are more useful signals than keyword overlap.
- Every resume claim should generate a specific interview question.
- Candidates should write resumes assuming every line will be tested, because it will be.
